## The Data Speaks: Card Analysis in La Liga (2005-2018)

In [Part 1](/blog/negreira-del-bayes-part-1), we learned to detect loaded dice using statistics and Bayes' Theorem. We saw how to:

- **Detect impossible patterns** with statistical tests (Chi-square, p-values)
- **Update beliefs** step by step with Bayes' Theorem
- **Understand quartiles** as a "4-sided die"

Now, we ask ourselves: **In which quartile was Barcelona during those 14 seasons of payments to Negreira?**

## The Quartile System (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4)

To analyze cards, we divide teams into **4 equal groups** each season:

- **Q1 (Quartile 1):** 25% of teams with **FEWER cards** (5 teams)
- **Q2 (Quartile 2):** Next 25% (5 teams)
- **Q3 (Quartile 3):** Next 25% (5 teams)
- **Q4 (Quartile 4):** 25% of teams with **MORE cards** (5 teams)

**Analogy:** It's exactly like our 4-sided die. Each team "rolls the die" each season and lands in one of the 4 quartiles.

**If everything is random**, we would expect each team to spend 25% of the time in each quartile.

### What's the Probability of This?

#### "4-Sided Die" Analysis (Quartiles)

Remember our 4-sided die from Part 1. If each season Barcelona "rolls the die" and can land on Q1, Q2, Q3 or Q4 (25% each):

```text
P(13 times in Q1 | random) = (0.25)^13 = 1.46 × 10⁻⁸
```

**This is 1 in 68 million**

For context:
- Probability of being struck by lightning in your lifetime: ~1 in 15,300
- Probability of winning the lottery: ~1 in 14 million
- **Probability of Barcelona in Q1 `13/13` times: 1 in 68 MILLION**

It's **almost 5 times more improbable** than winning the lottery.

#### "20-Sided Die" Analysis (Exact Rankings)

But there's more. Not only was Barcelona in Q1 (top 5 of 20), but they were **almost always in the first 2 places**.

Of the 13 seasons:
- **#1:** 9 times
- **#2:** 3 times
- **#3:** 1 time
- **#4 or worse:** 1 time

If each team has equal probability of each ranking (20-sided die), the probability of always being in the top 3 is:

```
P(12 times in top 3 | random) = (3/20)^12 = 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁰
```

**This is 1 in 7.7 BILLION**.

> The probability that any reader of this post will be struck by lightning in 2026 is 430 times greater than being in the top 3 least-carded teams in the league for 12 consecutive seasons.


### Statistical Test: Binomial Test

Now let's formalize this with rigorous statistics.

#### What is a Null Hypothesis?

In statistics, we always start by being **skeptical** and assuming that **nothing special** is happening. We call this the **null hypothesis (H₀)**.

**Analogy:** It's like in a trial - we assume innocence (null hypothesis) until evidence proves otherwise.

In our case:
- **H₀ (Null Hypothesis):** "Barcelona is like any other team. They have a 25% probability of landing in Q1 each season, randomly."
- **H₁ (Alternative Hypothesis):** "Barcelona is NOT random. There is something systematic favoring Q1."

#### What is the Binomial Test?

The **[binomial test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_test)** is perfect for our case because we're analyzing:

- A series of **independent events** (each season)
- Each event has **two possible outcomes** (in Q1 or not in Q1)
- Each event has a **fixed probability** (25% if random)

It's exactly like our 4-sided die from Part 1: each season we "roll the die" and ask "did it land on face '1' (Q1)?" If the die is fair, the probability is 25% each time.

#### Applying the Test

**Test question:** "If H₀ is true (Barcelona is random), what is the probability of observing `13/13` times in Q1?"

**Calculation:**
```text
P(13/13 in Q1 | H₀) = (0.25)^13 = 1.46 × 10⁻⁸
```

This number is called the **p-value**.

#### P-value Interpretation

These are the standard thresholds used in science to interpret p-values:

- **p > 0.05:** "Not enough evidence, could be chance" → We don't reject H₀
- **p < 0.05:** "Significant evidence, unlikely to be chance" → We reject H₀
  (This is the standard threshold in most scientific disciplines)
- **p < 0.01:** "Very strong evidence"
- **p < 0.001:** "Extremely strong evidence"
- **p < 0.0001:** "Practical certainty - virtually impossible to be chance"

**Our p-value: 1.46 × 10⁻⁸**

This is **14,600 times smaller** than the practical certainty threshold (p < 0.0001).

In scientific terms: **It is statistically impossible to be chance.**

#### Test Conclusion

**We reject the null hypothesis with >99.9999% confidence.**

In plain language: The probability that this is coincidence is **practically zero**.

> **There is something systematic causing Barcelona to land in Q1.**

But it's very important to make clear that, at this point: **We have no evidence that this systematic something is referee favoritism.** It could be playing style, cleanliness, or other intangibles. We cannot make the direct connection that this statistic, by itself, implies corrupt practices.

## Finding #3: Sigma Deviation - How "Rare" is Barcelona?

### The Context: Why Do We Need This Analysis?

So far we've seen that Barcelona was in Q1 `13/13` seasons. But someone could argue:

**"Sure, but SOMEONE has to be the best in this statistic. Isn't it normal for a big team like Barcelona to receive fewer cards?"**

**That's a good point.** In any league, there will always be a team that receives fewer cards. Being the best in a statistic doesn't automatically imply systematic favoritism.

**The key question is:** To what extent is it **normal** to be the best? And at what point do you become an **outlier** (atypical value) that falls outside what's expected?

To answer this, we need to understand **normal distributions** and **sigma deviations**.

### What is a Normal (Gaussian) Distribution?

Many natural phenomena follow a **[normal distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_distribution)** (also called Gaussian or "bell curve"):

![Normal curve with sigma deviations](/blog/negreira/Bell-curve.jpg)
*Figure 4: **Normal (Gaussian) distribution showing standard deviations (σ).** 68% of data falls within ±1σ, 95% within ±2σ, and 99.7% within ±3σ. Values beyond ±2σ are considered unusual, and beyond ±3σ are extreme outliers.*

**What does this curve tell us?**

- **Center (mean):** Where most of the data is
- **Standard deviations (σ):** Measure how far something is from the average
- **The empirical rule:**
  - **68%** of data is within ±1σ of the average (common)
  - **95%** is within ±2σ (uncommon outside this)
  - **99.7%** is within ±3σ (very rare outside this)

**Being the best is NOT necessarily abnormal.** But **being +3σ from the average** is.

### What is a Sigma Deviation?

In statistics, we measure how far a value is from the average using **standard deviations (σ)**:

- **±1σ:** Covers 68% of data (common)
- **±2σ:** Covers 95% of data (unusual)
- **±3σ:** Covers 99.7% of data (very rare)

**Analogy:** If average height is 170 cm with σ=10 cm:
- 160-180 cm (±1σ): Normal
- 150-190 cm (±2σ): Unusual but possible
- &lt;150 cm or &gt;190 cm (±3σ): Very rare

### Calculation for Barcelona - Yellow Cards

**League mean:** 97.9 cards/season
**Standard deviation:** 15.4 cards
**Barcelona:** 70.4 cards/season

```text
Z-score = (70.4 - 97.9) / 15.4 = -1.79σ
```

**Interpretation:** Barcelona is **1.79 standard deviations** below the mean.

This means only ~**5% of teams** receive as few cards as Barcelona.

**Is this evidence of favoritism?** By itself, **not necessarily**. Being in the lowest 5% is unusual, but not impossible by chance. Many phenomena have values in that range that are perfectly explainable by natural variability, team quality, playing style, etc.

## Bayesian Update #1: After the Q1 Finding

Now that we've seen the evidence that Barcelona was in Q1 `14/14` seasons, let's update our beliefs using **Bayes' Theorem**.

### Reminder: Our Prior

In Part 1, we established an **extremely skeptical prior**:

```text
P(referee bias) = 0.01% = 0.0001
```

That is, we start believing there's only **1 chance in 10,000** that bias exists.

### Applying Bayes with the Q1 Evidence

**Observed evidence:** Barcelona in Q1 `14/14` seasons

**Probabilities:**
1. **If there is bias** (referees favor Barcelona):
   - `P(14/14 in Q1 | bias) ≈ 0.80` (very likely to be in Q1)
2. **If there is NO bias** (everything is random):
   - `P(14/14 in Q1 | no bias) = (0.25)^14 = 3.73 × 10⁻⁹`

**We apply Bayes:**

```text
P(bias | 14/14 in Q1) = [P(14/14|bias) × P(bias)] / P(14/14)

P(14/14) = P(14/14|bias) × P(bias) + P(14/14|no bias) × P(no bias)
P(14/14) = (0.80 × 0.0001) + (3.73×10⁻⁹ × 0.9999)
P(14/14) ≈ 0.00008

P(bias | 14/14 in Q1) = [0.80 × 0.0001] / 0.00008
P(bias | 14/14 in Q1) ≈ 0.999 = 99.9%
```

### What Did We Just Calculate?

We started with **0.01% belief** in bias (ultra-skeptical).

After seeing that Barcelona was in Q1 `14/14` seasons, our belief updates to **99.9%**.

**Even being extremely skeptical at the start, the data forces us to change our mind.**

## Bayesian Update #2: Adding the Rivals Evidence

Now let's add the second piece of evidence: **Barcelona's rivals receive 1.51x more yellow cards**.

### How Improbable Is This?

If everything were random, we would expect the card ratio (Rivals/Team) to be close to **1.0** for all teams.

**We observe:**
- Barcelona: **1.51**
- Real Madrid: 1.22
- Atlético: 0.96
- Others: 0.99

To calculate the probability, we need the distribution of ratios in the league:

- **Mean of ratios:** 1.01 (almost perfect balance)
- **Standard deviation:** 0.18

**Barcelona's Z-score:**
```text
Z = (1.51 - 1.01) / 0.18 = 2.78σ
```

**Probability of a ratio ≥ 1.51 by chance:**
```text
P(Z ≥ 2.78) ≈ 0.0027 = 0.27%
```

That is, **1 in 370** teams would have such a high ratio by pure chance.

### Updating Bayes Again

Now we have **TWO independent pieces of evidence**:

1. Barcelona `14/14` in Q1 → P = 3.73 × 10⁻⁹
2. Rivals ratio 1.51 → P = 0.0027

**Combined probability of BOTH by chance:**
```text
P(both | no bias) = (3.73×10⁻⁹) × (0.0027) ≈ 1 × 10⁻¹¹
```

**This is 1 in 100 BILLION.**

Updating Bayes:
```text
P(bias | both evidences) > 99.99%
```

**There is no longer reasonable doubt.**

## The Busquets Case: When Statistics Become Reality

Sometimes, statistical anomalies have [concrete documented examples](https://www.futbolgate.com/investigaci%C3%B3n/el-d%C3%ADa-que-un-%C3%A1rbitro-sac%C3%B3-una-segunda-amarilla-a-busquets-pero-decidi%C3%B3-no-expulsarle).

In the 2009-10 season, Sergio Busquets (Barcelona) received a **second yellow card** in a match refereed by Teixeira Vitienes. However, the player was not sent off.

The situation repeated years later, in the last season analyzed in these documents, 2017-2018, with F.C. Barcelona player Gerard Piqué, who also received two yellow cards without being sent off the pitch.

Let's see what kind of statistical probability such flagrant errors have occurring twice, in the years when payments are documented, and both favoring the same team.

**Error Probability (P_error):**

We assume that the probability of a referee making this specific error in any match is tiny. It has happened 3 documented times in football history. The two known Barcelona cases and in 2006, at the World Cup, in the case of Josip Šimunić.

If we estimate 20 elite leagues, at 380 matches per year and 100 years of competition, we're talking about the possibility of this error occurring, and this is very conservative:

```text
P_error ~ 3 / 760,000 = 0.000003947
```

This happens once every 250,000 matches. The probability of this happening twice to the same team is then P_error^2 / total number of teams in those 20 leagues. Let's consider 40 teams per league over one hundred years. That is, 800 different teams. This means the probability of this happening to Barcelona is P_Barça = 1 / 800.

```text
P(Double Barça Coincidence) = (P_error × P_Barça)^2

P(Double Barça Coincidence) ~ (0.000003947 × (1/800))^2

P(Double Barça Coincidence) ~ 1 / 41,074,755,770,000,000
```

> The probability of this happening is one in 41 quadrillion, and it happened during those leaden years of payments.

## Summary of Evidence (So Far)

### Evidence #1: Q1 Quartiles
- **Fact:** Barcelona in Q1 `14/14` seasons
- **Probability by chance:** 1 in 268 million
- **P-value:** < 0.0001

### Evidence #2: Rivals Ratios
- **Fact:** Barcelona's rivals receive 1.51x more yellows, 1.98x more reds
- **Z-score:** 2.78σ (yellows)
- **Unique pattern:** Not seen in Real Madrid or Atlético, although a result below 3σ can be considered normal.

### Evidence #3: Deviation from Mean
- **Fact:** Barcelona is 1.79σ below the league mean
- **Interpretation:** Only ~5% of teams this low. However, a result below 3σ can be considered normal.

### Evidence #4: Double yellows without sending off
- **Fact:** Two Barcelona players receive a double yellow without being sent off
- **Interpretation:** This happening to the same team twice has a conservative probability of 1 in 41 quadrillion.

### Cumulative Bayesian Update

So far we have **THREE independent pieces of evidence**:

1. **Barcelona 14/14 in Q1** → P = 3.73 × 10⁻⁹ (1 in 268 million)
2. **Rivals ratio 1.51x in yellows** → P = 0.0027 (1 in 370)
3. **Busquets/Piqué Case: Double referee error** → P = 2.43 × 10⁻¹⁷ (1 in 41 quadrillion)

**Combined probability of all THREE evidences by chance:**
```text
P(all | no bias) = (3.73×10⁻⁹) × (0.0027) × (2.43×10⁻¹⁷) ≈ 2.4×10⁻²⁷
```

**This is 1 in 400 SEXTILLION.**

**Bayesian Update:**
- **Prior:** 0.01% (ultra-skeptical)
- **Posterior:** >99.999999999999% (virtually certain, 15 nines)

**Even starting extremely skeptical, the data forces us to conclude there is a non-random pattern.**

## But... Could There Be Other Explanations?

You've seen the figures. You've seen the probabilities. Bayes' Theorem tells us there's >99.99% certainty of a non-random pattern.

**But let's be fair:** Before concluding something so serious, we must consider all possible alternative explanations.

### Alternative Hypotheses


#### Hypothesis #1: "Barcelona has more possession, commits fewer fouls"

**Argument:** Barcelona's famous "tiki-taka" gives them more possession, therefore they commit fewer fouls and receive fewer cards.

**Problems with this hypothesis:**

- ✅ Could explain Barcelona's **few own cards**
- ❌ Does NOT explain why Barcelona's **RIVALS** receive 1.51x more yellow cards
- ❌ Does NOT explain why Barcelona's rivals receive 1.98x more red cards
- Real Madrid's rivals also face a team with lots of possession, but their ratio is only 1.22x
- **Why are Barcelona's rivals punished more harshly than other big teams' rivals?**

#### Hypothesis #2: "Barcelona has better players who draw more fouls"

**Argument:** Messi, Iniesta, Xavi were so good that rivals committed more fouls against them.

**Problems with this hypothesis:**

- Real Madrid had Cristiano Ronaldo, Benzema, Modric, Kroos - players of equivalent quality
- Real Madrid also drew fouls, but ratio of 1.22 vs Barcelona's 1.51
- **Why would Barcelona draw 25% more fouls than Real Madrid?**
- Atlético also had world-class players, but ratio of only 0.96x
- **Doesn't explain the magnitude of the difference**

#### Hypothesis #3: "It's the specific playing style of certain coaches"

**Argument:** Guardiola and Luis Enrique had a style that generated this pattern.

**Problems with this hypothesis:**

- The pattern spans **13 seasons** with multiple very different coaches:
  - **Frank Rijkaard** (2005-2008): 3 seasons - Q1 all 3 times
  - **Pep Guardiola** (2008-2012): 4 seasons - Q1 all 4 times
  - **Tito Vilanova** (2012-2013): 1 season - Q1
  - **Tata Martino** (2013-2014): 1 season - Q1
  - **Luis Enrique** (2014-2017): 3 seasons - Q1 all 3 times (Who has stated he never saw any referee reports)
  - **Ernesto Valverde** (2017-2018): 1 season - Q1 (Who has stated he never saw any referee reports)
- **6 different coaches, different styles, but same pattern**
- **The probability that 6 different coaches produce the same extreme result by chance is astronomically low**

### The Question We Cannot Ignore

All these alternative hypotheses have a fundamental problem. None explains:

1. The **perfect consistency** (`13/13` seasons in Q1, probability 1 in 68 million)
2. Barcelona's **unique pattern** (not replicated by Real Madrid or Atlético)
3. The **extreme ratios** of cards to rivals (1.51x yellows, 1.98x reds)
4. The **coach independence** (6 different managers, same result)

**So... is it possible that all these are coincidences? That Barcelona simply got "lucky" to have the perfect style, the perfect coaches, at the perfect moment, for 13 consecutive years?**

Possible, yes. **Probable, as the statistics demonstrate, no.**

### The Definitive Test: A Natural Experiment

But there's a way to resolve this conclusively.

**The key question is:**

Is this pattern about **Barcelona as a team** (quality, style, discipline)?

Is this pattern **related to payments to the vice president of the referees committee**?

If the pattern is about **Barcelona as a team**, it should appear **in all competitions** where Barcelona plays.

If the pattern is **related to payments to the vice president of the referees committee**, it should appear **only in La Liga** and disappear with referees independent of Negreira.

**How can we test this?**

We would need a competition where:

1. ✅ Barcelona and Real Madrid participate
2. ✅ They play against teams of similar quality
3. ✅ But the referees are **international**, not Spanish
4. ✅ The referees have no connection to Negreira or the Spanish Referees Committee

**Does such a competition exist?**

Yes.

It's called **The UEFA Champions League**.

In the Champions League:

- The same teams (Barcelona, Real Madrid)
- The same players (Messi, Cristiano)
- The same coaches (Guardiola, Mourinho, Luis Enrique, Zidane)
- The same playing styles (tiki-taka, possession, high pressing)
- **But completely different referees** - appointed by UEFA, not by Spain

**If Barcelona's pattern disappears in the Champions League, that would prove it's NOT about the team.**

**If the pattern persists, then there's something intrinsic to Barcelona that causes this.**

We've analyzed 13 seasons of La Liga. We've seen probabilities of 1 in 68 million. We've updated our Bayesian beliefs to >99.99%.

But a reasonable doubt remains: **What if all the alternative explanations, combined, somehow produce this extraordinary pattern?**

It's improbable. Extremely improbable. But not impossible....

**What do you think we're going to find?**
